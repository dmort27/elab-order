Now, 35 years later, Niam Tij Teeb came to visit us. I had the chance
to talk to my sister-in-law face to face and openly. The very own
sister-in-law whom once, back in Laos belonged to the bourgeoisie class
in which at that time, I wouldn't dare talk to or look at. I simply
knew her as my "Niam Tij". After a long talk, I dared ask her, why
she had to leave my brother? Surprisingly, she looked at me with an
angry face and replied in french:-" Le tetu, je lui ai deja explique,
le Laos allait etre change'. Il n'a pas voulu m"ecoute!". I saw
tears coming out from a such a strong woman who had raised four kids
all by herself. A moment later, I asked her if she had been married.
She replied without a word. She showed me her passport bearing, indeed,
the Lynhiavu as last name.  This simple gesture of her loyalty warmed
my heart and truly showed her everlasting love for my brother.  After
another silence, she looked at me and said, "Now that all the kids
grew up well educated and all have good jobs, it is now your turn to
take them back to the Lynhiavu's family. I will go take care of my
aging mother in Laos"....She just left for  France before returning
to Laos.
