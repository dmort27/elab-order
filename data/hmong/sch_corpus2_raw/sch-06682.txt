Three Hmong girls reportedly prostituted; community demands action
Paul Gustafson,  Star Tribune
May 28, 2004HMONG0528
?/span>
?/span>
Citing details of a recent prostitution investigation, Hmong community
leaders and law enforcement officials in St. Paul called Thursday for
new efforts to stop sexual exploitation of runaway Hmong girls.
The investigation found that three Hmong girls younger than 15 were
repeatedly prostituted to Hmong men. Two St. Paul residents received
prison sentences in the case.
Using the case as an illustration of a growing problem, Ramsey County
Attorney Susan Gaertner said law enforcement and social service
agencies and the Hmong community must combat the problem "before
[runaway Hmong girls] become throw-away children." She promised "a
very public dialogue" about the issue. The three girls, one age 12,
had sexually transmitted diseases, and were victims of "too many
sexual assaults to quantify," reported a nurse at the Midwest
Children's Resource Center in St. Paul.
The nurse, who often deals with sexually abused children, scheduled
appointments to talk to the girls on different days because their
stories "were too dreadful to hear [all] at one time," Gaertner said.
Although the problem of runaway girls being sexually exploited cuts
across all cultures, authorities said they are concerned about several
recent cases involving prostitution of Hmong girls.
Authorities said they received strong cooperation from the Hmong
community during their investigation into the exploitation of the
three girls.
They acknowledged, however, that getting some Hmong victims and their
families to cooperate in such cases can be difficult because of the
stigma placed on rape victims by some Hmong community members. Hmong
leaders and public officials said they hope a new Hmong Youth Task
Force in St. Paul can find "proactive" ways to address the issue.
Gaertner pointed to an effort by the Midwest Children's Resource
Center to get federal funding for a new program to work with runaway
Hmong and their families.
LiCho Xenexai, vice president of the Hmong 18 Council, composed of
leaders of the 18 Hmong clans, called on his community "to assume our
responsibility to support the task force and cooperate with law
enforcement" to stop sexual exploitation of Hmong girls.
An investigation by the Minnesota Gang Strike Force led to felony
charges of prostituting a minor against two St. Paul residents, Tha
Ying Moua, 18, and Marla Thao, 21. Both were sentenced to 11 years and
one month in prison.
Two of the girls told a Strike Force investigator that they stayed at
Thao's apartment in the 700 block of Jessie St. while they were
runaways in 2003, according to a criminal complaint.
One girl said that Thao and Moua took them in June 2003 to the
Northernaire Motel in Maplewood, where they prostituted the girls for
multiple men.
The girls said Thao and Moua also prostituted them at Thao's apartment
several times in the fall of 2003 with several men.
Ten Hmong men, ranging in age from 36 to 59, allegedly had sex with
one of the girls. Each has been charged with hiring a minor for
prostitution, a felony.
Authorities also noted the cases of two 15-year-old Hmong girls from
River Falls, Wis., who were brought to the Twin Cities and prostituted
in April 2003. The U.S. attorney's office in Minnesota obtained
indictments against five men in the cases, and all have pleaded
guilty.
Gang Strike Force investigator Kevin Navara said that more than 80
Hmong girls have been reported victims of rape and prostitution since
1998, and that more than 200 suspects have been identified during
investigation of those reports.
Ilean Her, executive director of the Council on Asian-Pacific
Minnesotans, said some Hmong girls and their families are reluctant to
admit a rape or cooperate with police because they fear being
stigmatized in their community.
Some Hmong families will even seek to have their daughters married to
the men who sexually exploited them, Her said.
In some Hmong families, teenage girls raised in the United States want
to have more freedom than they are allowed by parents raised in
traditional Hmong culture, she said.
For example, Her said, a girl may not understand why her parents
object to her attending a school dance. But the parents may not
understand their daughter's wants because they were raised in a
culture where girls married at an early age and had no teenage years
of freedom, she said.
Hmong parents "are changing ... but they are not changing as fast as
their kids," Her said.
Dr. Carolyn Levitt, executive director of the Midwest Children's
Resource Center, said she believes that efforts to reconcile runaway
girls and their parents can succeed.
While parents and girls might clash over cultural issues, Hmong
runaways tend not to be as alienated from their parents as other
runaways. The more alienated runaways seem to have more drug problems
or are more frequently fleeing physical or sexual abuse at home,
Levitt said.
She said her center hopes to submit a federal grant application soon
to hire a nurse practitioner, a public health nurse and several case
workers for a program to reconcile Hmong runaways with their families.
